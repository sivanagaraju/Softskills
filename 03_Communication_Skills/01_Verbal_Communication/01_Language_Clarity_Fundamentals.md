## 1. Active Voice vs. Passive Voice

**Active voice** makes your communication direct and confident. The subject performs the action.
**Passive voice** hides the doer and sounds vague. Use it only when the doer is unknown or deliberately de-emphasized.

### The Rule

> **Active:** `[Who] + [did what] + [to whom/what]`
> **Passive:** `[What] + [was done] + [by whom]` â† the "by whom" often gets dropped, creating confusion.

### Quick Conversion

To convert passive â†’ active, ask: **"Who is doing this?"** Then put that person first.

### Corporate Examples

| âŒ Passive (Vague) | âœ… Active (Clear) | Why It's Better |
|---|---|---|
| "The deployment was completed." | "We completed the deployment." | Reader knows **who** did it |
| "The issue has been escalated." | "I escalated the issue to the infra team." | Shows **ownership** and **to whom** |
| "It was decided to postpone the release." | "The team decided to postpone the release." | No mystery about the decision-maker |
| "The requirements are being gathered." | "The BA team is gathering the requirements." | Clear accountability |
| "Mistakes were made in the estimate." | "We underestimated the effort by two sprints." | Takes responsibility, sounds mature |
| "You will be updated." | "I will update you by Friday." | Specific commitment, not a vague promise |

### When Passive Voice IS Appropriate

| Situation | Example | Why Passive Works Here |
|---|---|---|
| The doer is unknown | "The server was accessed at 2 AM." | We don't know who |
| Diplomatic/sensitive situations | "The deadline was missed." | Avoids blaming someone publicly |
| Formal compliance language | "Data must be encrypted at rest." | Focus is on the requirement, not the person |

### ðŸ—£ï¸ Speaking Tip

When speaking, passive voice creates **filler pauses** because your brain searches for the subject. Active voice flows naturally: *"I did X, the team handled Y, we shipped Z."*

## 2. Sentence Structure

Three types. Each has a purpose. Mixing them keeps your communication dynamic.

### Simple Sentence â€” One idea, one clause

> "We deployed the fix." / "The sprint ends Friday."

**Use for:** Key facts, action items, status updates. Short sentences punch.

### Compound Sentence â€” Two equal ideas joined by a connector

> "We deployed the fix, **and** the issue is resolved."
> "The timeline is tight, **but** we can meet the deadline."

**Connectors:** and, but, so, yet, or

**Use for:** Linking cause-effect or showing contrast in updates.

### Complex Sentence â€” One main idea + one dependent clause

> "**Since** the client approved the design, we can begin development."
> "We'll proceed with Option A **unless** you have concerns."

**Starters:** since, because, although, unless, when, if, after, before, while

**Use for:** Giving context before a decision or action.

### Corporate Examples

| âŒ Run-On / Messy | âœ… Structured | Type Used |
|---|---|---|
| "We finished the testing and the results are good and we can deploy now so let me know." | "Testing is complete, and all results are positive. We're ready to deploy â€” please confirm." | Compound + Simple |
| "Because we had issues with the API and the vendor didn't respond and we lost two days." | "We lost two days because the vendor didn't respond to our API issue." | Complex (one clear cause) |
| "I think we should go with option B it makes more sense cost-wise and the team prefers it too." | "I recommend Option B. It's more cost-effective, and the team prefers it." | Simple + Compound |

### ðŸ—£ï¸ Speaking Tip

**Speak in short sentences.** Long compound sentences become rambling when spoken. Break them at every "and" / "so." Pause. Then start the next sentence fresh.

## 5. Tense Consistency

Jumping between tenses within a message confuses the reader. Pick one timeframe per section and stick to it.

### The Three Tenses You'll Use Most

| Tense | When to Use | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Past** (completed) | Reporting what happened | "We **completed** testing." / "The issue **was resolved**." |
| **Present** (ongoing) | Describing current state | "The build **is running**." / "We **are waiting** for approval." |
| **Future** (planned) | Stating next steps | "We **will deploy** tomorrow." / "I **will share** the report by EOD." |

### The Mistake

| âŒ Mixed Tenses | âœ… Consistent Tenses |
|---|---|
| "We **completed** testing and the build **is running** and we **deploy** tomorrow." | "We **completed** testing. The build **is running** now. We **will deploy** tomorrow." |
| "I **sent** the email and **am waiting** for response and will **update** you." | "I **sent** the email and **am** awaiting a response. I'll **update** you once I hear back." |

### The Simple Rule

> When reporting status, use this natural flow:
> **Past** (what's done) â†’ **Present** (where we are now) â†’ **Future** (what's next)
>
> Separate them into distinct sentences. Don't chain them with "and."

### ðŸ—£ï¸ Speaking Tip

In standups, follow this exact flow: *"Yesterday I **did** X. Today I **am working** on Y. I **will** have Z ready by tomorrow."* Three tenses, three sentences, clean.

## 6. Subject-Verb Agreement

Sounds basic, but agreement errors are common in corporate writing, especially with collective nouns and compound subjects.

### Quick Rules

| Rule | âŒ Wrong | âœ… Correct |
|---|---|---|
| Singular subject â†’ singular verb | "The team **are** working on it." | "The team **is** working on it." |
| Compound subject â†’ plural verb | "The dev and QA **is** ready." | "The dev and QA teams **are** ready." |
| "Each" / "Every" â†’ singular | "Each of the modules **are** tested." | "Each of the modules **is** tested." |
| "None" â†’ usually singular | "None of the issues **are** critical." | "None of the issues **is** critical." (formal) |
| Words between subject and verb don't change agreement | "The list of items **are** ready." | "The list of items **is** ready." |

### Corporate Tip

> When in doubt, simplify the sentence. Instead of *"The list of deliverables that were reviewed by the team are ready,"* write: *"The reviewed deliverables are ready."*

## 7. Modifiers & Ambiguity

A **modifier** is a word or phrase that describes something. When it's placed wrong, it describes the *wrong* thing â€” creating confusion or unintentional humor.

### Dangling Modifiers

The modifier has no clear subject to attach to.

| âŒ Dangling | âœ… Clear | What Went Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| "After reviewing the code, **the bug was found.**" | "After reviewing the code, **I found the bug.**" | The bug didn't review the code |
| "By deploying early, **the risk was reduced.**" | "By deploying early, **we reduced the risk.**" | The risk didn't deploy |
| "While testing the API, **an error occurred.**" | "While **I was** testing the API, an error occurred." | The error didn't test the API |

### Misplaced Modifiers

The modifier is in the wrong position, changing the meaning.

| âŒ Misplaced | âœ… Correct | Meaning Change |
|---|---|---|
| "I **only** need approval from the client." | "I need approval **only** from the client." | "Only" should modify "client," not "need" |
| "We **almost** fixed all the bugs." | "We fixed **almost** all the bugs." | "Almost" should modify "all," not "fixed" |
| "Please review the document **quickly** shared by the team." | "Please quickly review the document shared by the team." | "Quickly" should modify "review," not "shared" |

### The Fix

> Always place the modifier **next to** the word it describes. Read the sentence aloud â€” if it sounds odd, the modifier is probably in the wrong place.

## 8. Connectors & Transitions

Connectors signal the **relationship** between your ideas. Using the right connector eliminates ambiguity.

### Connector Cheat Sheet

| Relationship | Connectors | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Addition** | also, additionally, furthermore, moreover | "Additionally, we need to update the staging environment." |
| **Contrast** | however, but, although, on the other hand, yet | "However, the timeline may need adjustment." |
| **Cause** | because, since, due to, as a result of | "Since the client changed requirements, we need to re-scope." |
| **Effect / Result** | therefore, consequently, as a result, so, hence | "Therefore, I recommend we extend the sprint by two days." |
| **Sequence** | first, then, next, finally, subsequently | "First, we'll complete testing. Then, we'll deploy to staging." |
| **Condition** | if, unless, provided that, in case | "Unless we get feedback by Wednesday, we'll proceed with the current design." |
| **Summary** | in summary, to summarize, overall, in short | "In summary, we're on track with minor adjustments needed." |
| **Example** | for example, for instance, such as, specifically | "For example, the login module throws a timeout error." |

### Common Mistakes

| âŒ Wrong Connector | âœ… Right Connector | Why |
|---|---|---|
| "The build failed, **and** we need to investigate." | "The build failed, **so** we need to investigate." | It's a cause-effect, not just addition |
| "We have resources, **so** the timeline is tight." | "We have resources, **but** the timeline is tight." | It's a contrast, not a result |
| "**Because** of the delay, **because** the vendor was late." | "The delay occurred **because** the vendor was late." | One "because" is enough |

### ðŸ—£ï¸ Speaking Tip

In speech, **pause** where a connector goes. Instead of chaining with "and... and... and...", use: *"We completed testing. [pause] However, we found two issues. [pause] So, we need one more day."*

## 9. Tone Register

The same message sounds different depending on tone. Match tone to audience and situation.

### The Three Registers

| Register | When to Use | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **Casual** | Slack with teammates, 1-on-1 with close colleagues | Contractions, short sentences, emojis OK, first names |
| **Professional** | Client emails, stakeholder updates, cross-team comms | Clear, polite, structured, no slang, appropriate formality |
| **Formal** | Escalations, legal/compliance, C-level communication | No contractions, precise language, diplomatic framing |

### Same Message, Three Tones

**Situation:** Informing someone about a deployment issue.

| Register | Message |
|---|---|
| **Casual** | "Hey, heads up â€” deploy hit a snag. Looking into it, should be sorted in an hour." |
| **Professional** | "Hi [Name], I wanted to flag that we encountered an issue during deployment. We're investigating and expect resolution within the hour. I'll keep you updated." |
| **Formal** | "Dear [Name], I am writing to inform you that an issue was identified during the scheduled deployment. Our team is actively investigating the root cause, and we anticipate resolution within approximately one hour. We will provide a status update upon resolution." |

### Tone Danger Zones

| Risk | âŒ Example | âœ… Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Too casual with client | "Yeah, we messed up. My bad." | "I apologize for the oversight. Here's how we're addressing it." |
| Too formal with teammate | "Dear colleague, I wish to request your assistance." | "Hey, can you help me with this?" |
| Aggressive disguised as direct | "You need to fix this immediately." | "Could you prioritize this? We need a fix by EOD." |
| Passive-aggressive | "Per my last email..." / "As I mentioned before..." | "To clarify..." / "Just to recap..." |
